# Steve Jobs — Taste, Focus, and Saying No

## Core Principles

- **Focus is about saying no:** Jobs killed 70% of Apple's product line when he returned in 1997. The remaining 30% became the most valuable company in history. The lesson: excellence comes from SUBTRACTION, not addition. Every strategy Dae adds is a potential dilution of focus on the strategies that work.
- **Simplicity is the ultimate sophistication:** The iPod beat every MP3 player not with more features but with fewer. One button. One interface. One purpose. For Dae: calibration_edge succeeds because it does ONE THING (exploit favorite-longshot bias) with precision. Adding complexity to a strategy that works is the path to mediocrity.
- **Taste:** Jobs talked about the intersection of technology and liberal arts — machines with soul. For Dae: a trading system with "taste" means knowing WHICH trades are elegant and WHICH are ugly. An elegant trade has a clear thesis, wide margin of safety, and asymmetric payoff. An ugly trade has a muddled thesis, thin margin, and symmetric risk. Reject ugly trades even if they have positive EV.
- **Skate to where the puck is going:** Don't trade where the market IS. Trade where the market is GOING. The forward signal bridge IS this principle — reading prediction market price velocity as a leading indicator of where capital markets will move.
- **Reality distortion field (used wisely):** Jobs's ability to believe in outcomes that seemed impossible and will them into existence. For Dae: this is NOT about ignoring reality. It's about setting audacious compounding targets and engineering the system to achieve them. "$159 to generational wealth" is a reality distortion field — but one backed by mathematics (Kelly + compounding + edge).
- **The product is the strategy:** Jobs didn't hire great marketers to sell mediocre products. He built products so good they marketed themselves. Dae shouldn't need clever risk management to make a bad strategy profitable. The STRATEGY should be so good that risk management is guardrails, not crutches.

## Mental Models

- **The Quadrant:** When Jobs returned to Apple, he drew a 2x2 grid: Consumer/Professional x Desktop/Portable. Four products. That's it. For Dae's strategy portfolio:

  | | Prediction Markets | Capital Markets |
  |---|---|---|
  | **Proven Edge** | calibration_edge, high_probability_bonds | (awaiting graduation) |
  | **Speculative Edge** | (disabled, proving ground) | crisis_alpha, stock_momentum |

  Each quadrant gets at most 2-3 strategies. The total portfolio should fit on a napkin.

- **The Integrated Product:** Jobs insisted that hardware, software, and services work together seamlessly. Dae's equivalent: the strategy, the governance engine, the capital allocator, and the risk system must be an INTEGRATED SYSTEM — not independent components bolted together. A governance decision should flow seamlessly into an allocation adjustment, which flows into a position size change, which flows into a trade execution.

- **Prototype, Demo, Ship:** Jobs's product cycle was relentless: build a prototype (paper trading), demo it (arena tournament), ship it (graduate to live). The transition from prototype to ship must be fast and decisive. Dwelling in paper mode too long is the Apple III — a product that never shipped.

- **Kill Your Darlings:** Jobs killed the Newton, killed the Cube, killed products HE created when they weren't working. The lesson: ego has no place in portfolio management. If a strategy isn't working, kill it. Even if you spent 40 hours building it. Even if the thesis was brilliant. Dead strategies are not failures — they're taste in action.

## Prediction Market Translation

- **Focused strategy selection:** Jobs would look at 19 strategies and say "this is a mess." He'd keep calibration_edge (the iPhone — one product that defines the company), high_probability_bonds (the Mac — reliable, steady), and maybe crisis_alpha (the iPad — different form factor for different conditions). Everything else gets killed until these three are perfected.
- **The integrated Dae experience:** Strategy → Governance → Allocation → Execution → Learning should be ONE seamless flow. No manual intervention. No watching. No overriding. The system should be so well-integrated that Eddie can check once a day and everything is running.
- **Saying no to trades:** Most opportunities should be rejected. Not because they're bad — but because they're not GREAT. Jobs rejected thousands of good ideas to make room for the few great ones. Dae should reject 95% of positive-EV opportunities to concentrate on the 5% with the widest edge.
- **Taste in trade selection:** An elegant Kalshi trade: 85c contract with true probability 94%, 9c edge, settling in 3 days, regime confirms, allocator has weight. An ugly Kalshi trade: 52c contract with estimated probability 55%, 3c edge, settling in 30 days, regime is uncertain. Both have positive EV. Jobs would only take the first.

## When This Applies

- **Regime alignment:** LOW_VOL_CALM (the environment rewards focus and patience — Apple's best products were built in calm, focused environments), MEAN_REVERTING (simplicity wins when complexity fails)
- **Signal:** Too many strategies competing for capital (need to simplify), strategies with unclear edge mechanisms (need to cut), portfolio complexity increasing without corresponding returns (need the quadrant exercise)
- **Anti-signal:** HIGH_VOL_CHOPPY (chaos requires adaptability, not focus), TRENDING strongly (momentum requires riding many positions, not concentrating)

## Capital Phase Alignment

- **SEED:** "Focus and simplicity. That's been one of my mantras." At SEED, you ARE Apple-1997. Kill everything except the 2 products that work. Run calibration_edge and high_probability_bonds. Nothing else.
- **GROWTH:** Add ONE new product per cycle. Test it in paper mode. If it's not great, kill it. If it's great, ship it (graduate).
- **FOUNDATION:** The integrated product. At this scale, the system should run as one seamless machine. No manual overrides. No exceptions.
- **DYNASTY:** "People think focus means saying yes to the thing you've got to focus on. But that's not what it means at all. It means saying no to the hundred other good ideas." At DYNASTY, the portfolio is SMALL and EXCELLENT — not large and adequate.
